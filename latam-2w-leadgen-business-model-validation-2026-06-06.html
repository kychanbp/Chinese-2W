Bottom line up front

The model is real — but marginal and conversion-gated
Verdict

A viable per-lead price band barely exists at base assumptions, and the simple "sell leads to dealers" version only clears at high conversion + low CAC. A dealer rationally pays at most conversion × profit-per-sale ÷ ROI-hurdle per lead. On a ~$2,200 Chinese commuter (~$330 dealer margin) at 6% conversion that's $6.60/lead — barely above our ~$5 cost to produce one. Below ~4% conversion the band is empty.

The model

Why absolute price matters

Normalized view — % of retail, and the price floor

The dealer side is unit-independent: a dealer's max willingness-to-pay is a fixed fraction of the bike's price — conversion × margin ÷ ROI = 0.30% of retail at base ratios, so per-lead revenue scales linearly with the bike. But your CAC (~$5) and fixed cost ($6k/mo) are absolute dollars that don't scale — so there's a minimum bike price below which 0.30% of it can't even cover the $5 it costs to make the lead, and the dealer-only band is empty.

The price floor. Dealer-only viable only when 0.30% × price > $5 → price > ~$1,667. Financing origination is also price-proportional (% of loan), lifting price-scaled revenue to ~0.39% → floor drops to ~$1,294. Below these, no per-lead price works for both sides. The model structurally favours pricier / higher-margin bikes.

Both revenue lines start at $0 and rise with bike price; the $5 CAC is flat. Where each line crosses CAC is its break-even bike price (dots): $1,667 dealer-only, $1,294 with financing. Left of those, revenue/lead sits in the red loss zone. The $2,200 base is just past the dealer-only floor — viable but thin.

The real ceiling

Make vs buy — what a dealer's own customer acquisition actually costs

The dealer's ROI hurdle is only the theoretical price ceiling. The binding ceiling is what it costs them to get the same buyer themselves — because they'll never pay you more than their own make cost. So price ≤ min(ROI limit, dealer's own CAC), and only for the leads their free organic channels don't already supply. A 4-agent research sweep put real numbers on it:

The arbitrage-compression problem. You and the dealer buy from the same Meta auction, so your raw lead cost ≈ their raw lead cost (~$5 each). The pure per-lead spread is therefore near zero. The business only exists three ways: (1) beat an amateur dealer's cost (they run ads badly → ~$8–10, you hit ~$5 → a real ~$3–5 arbitrage); (2) win on speed-to-lead conversion (fast WhatsApp ~20% vs their slow ~6%); (3) the finance-qualification + origination layer they can't replicate. What the model forces

Re-pointed thesis — three ways the economics get un-marginal

The thin band on "cheap leads → small dealer → cheap bike" points to higher-value variants. These become the real hypotheses to test:

1
Sell to BRANDS, not small dealers
A new Chinese entrant values market-entry + brand-building + trust above pure per-lead ROI — so it can pay above a dealer's ROI hurdle. Matches the "long-tail entrants need demand-gen" finding.
2
Capture the financing-origination fee
A finance-qualified lead pays twice — dealer and lender/insurer. This is why banks own Brazil's auto classifieds. The highest-value layer and the real moat.
3
Skew to higher-margin bikes
Premium Chinese (CFMoto, QJ/Benelli) carry $500–1,000+ dealer margin vs ~$330 — lifting WTP 2–3× and widening the band.
Re-pointed thesis. Not "cheap leads for cheap-bike dealers" (marginal) — but finance-qualified leads sold to demand-gen-hungry Chinese brands, monetized again on loan origination, skewed to higher-margin models. Slightly less asset-light, but where the economics actually clear.

Why the model works

The revenue stack — one buyer, three payers

The model holds because a single acquired buyer is sold — in different forms — to three different payers, each buying a different thing. This is aggregator economics: you own the scarce asset (the buyer relationship), and every party in the chain that needs access to that buyer pays you for it. Crucially, only one of the three slices — the dealer fee — is the thin, arbitraged layer the earlier sections worried about; the other two escape it.

① BRAND pays for demand
$1–3k/mo retainer + market-entry package + demand-data product. A new Chinese entrant's goal is market entry & awareness, not per-lead ROI → pays above any hurdle. Stickiest, most predictable revenue; less asset-light but non-arbitraged.
② DEALER pays for the buyer
$4–8 / qualified lead, capped at min(ROI limit, their self-serve CAC). The thin, arbitraged slice (you both buy the same Meta auction). Role: cover your CAC — not where the profit is.
③ LENDER pays for the loan
$45–175 / loan origination (2–5%) + insurance attach (~10–20%). Paid from interest margin → uncapped by the dealer's hurdle. Highest per-transaction margin — the real moat (why banks own the auto classifieds).
The catch — financing rivals the dealer. Financing is normally the dealer's F&I profit center, so you can't naively stack stream ③ on top of ②. Capture it by pre-approving the buyer upstream (through your lender partner, before the dealer handoff, so the loan originates on your rail) or by selling the qualified lead straight to a lender. It's easiest exactly where the dealer is weak at F&I — small/new-brand dealers, and thin-file buyers your specialist lender approves when the dealer's bank won't (you create a loan, not steal margin). That's the same ICP as the dealer-fee opportunity.

How it ties to the unit economics: stream ② alone is the marginal/negative base case in the calculator. Stream ③ (financing) is what flips CM positive; stream ① (brand) escapes the ROI-hurdle ceiling entirely. The deepest version is buyer lifetime value — insurance renewals, the next-bike upgrade, refinancing, referrals — which only works if you keep the WhatsApp relationship instead of handing it off. One buyer, three payers, recurring over time: that is the difference between a per-lead grind and a business.

The test

90-day, $3–5k kill-or-confirm plan

Spend on learning, not building. Each phase has a hard kill criterion so a dead idea dies in weeks, not years.

Phase 1 · Wk 1–3 · tests R1,R4

Demand interviews

15–20 structured interviews: long-tail Chinese-brand importers, independent multi-brand dealers, 1–2 lenders/consórcio admins. The three questions that extract the real price ceiling (their make-cost, not the ROI hurdle):

  • "What % of your sales are walk-in / referral vs paid ads vs marketplace?" → your incremental TAM
  • "Do you run your own Meta/Google ads? What's your cost per buyer?" → your real price ceiling
  • "What do you pay Webmotors / Mercado Libre per lead now?" → market reference
Phase 2 · Wk 3–9 · tests R2,R3

Concierge MVP / smoke test

One city (São Paulo or Bogotá). Neutral comparison page + social; ~$1–2k CTWA ads for ONE brand; hand-qualify over WhatsApp; route free to 3–5 dealers with a tracking handshake. Measure the real lead→sale conversion (call dealers weekly).

Phase 3 · Wk 9–13 · tests R1 for real

Invoice for real money

Go back to dealers/brands who got free leads and charge them — per-lead or per-sale success fee — at 2–3 price points inside the modeled band. Measure free→paid conversion and churn.

Total cash at risk ~$3–5k (mostly ad spend + your time). The point is to reach a confident go/no-go on R1+R2 before building any product.
