Bottom line up front

The recommendation
Decision

The opportunity is the whole Chinese 2W "going-global" wave — petrol (ICE) and electric — not EV alone. Chinese 2W exports are growing 30–45%/yr, fastest into Latin America (+55%) and Africa (+73%). For the asset-light consumer lead-gen model you chose, that opens two viable Stage-1 beachheads: Malaysia-EV (cleanest fit, small) or Brazil-ICE (bigger, riding the Shineray category surge).

Final synthesis (after 4 theaters + unit economics): LatAm (Brazil/Colombia) is the best shape — the value chain is open, Chinese brands partner-distribute, and Brazil has a proven per-lead economy. Africa is biggest-growth but captured (a capital play, not lead-gen). Malaysia-EV is clean but tiny. But the unit-economics model (§Validation) shows even the LatAm asset-light per-lead version is marginal and conversion-gated — viable only via finance-qualified leads to brands + loan-origination, at conversion ≥ ~8%. So the decision is not "build" — it's run the $3–5k / 90-day field test to prove dealers/brands pay and leads convert, before committing. See LatAm theater and Validation.

The lens

Market-Entry Framework — five MECE modules

Governing thesis: the value is not in the product or the lead — it is in whichever layer resolves the buyer's binding constraint. Match the business model to where demand is real, then sequence entry from cheapest-to-prove to highest-margin.

Size it

How big, how fast, how real?
Volume × penetration × growth — and whether growth sits on a meaningful base or a tiny one.

Segment demand

Who actually buys, and why now?
Fleet/B2B vs. retail consumer; regulation vs. subsidy as the trigger.

Map the value chain

Where does value leak or concentrate?
What the foreign supplier cannot provide locally — the binding constraint you could own.

Pick the model

What can you own that's defensible?
Score business-model variants against disintermediation risk.

Sequence entry

Where first, and in what order?
Maturity × white-space × model-fit. Enter the smallest market where the model is cleanest to validate; the audience/relationship asset becomes the on-ramp to bigger or heavier plays.

But the branded leaders self-distribute (Shineray 438 stores), so the lead-gen sweet spot is the mid-tier brands + not-yet-entered markets without local networks.

Module 2

Segment demand — fleet-first, and regulation > subsidy

Who buys

EV adoption is led by ride-hailing and last-mile delivery fleets everywhere — high daily mileage makes EV TCO + battery-swap economics work (swapping = 20–40% more daily orders). Private retail EV demand is meaningful only in Vietnam and Malaysia.

MarketDominant EV buyer
Indonesia · BrazilFleet / gig (B2B)
VietnamRetail + mandated ride-hail
MalaysiaRetail private consumer

Why now — the demand switch

Regulation creates demand floors; subsidies create demand cliffs.

  • Bans (durable): Vietnam's Hanoi/HCMC ICE bans drove EV to ~22%.
  • Subsidies (fragile): Indonesia & Malaysia rebates both lapsed, producing demand cliffs.

Weight a market's policy by its durability, not its generosity. Malaysia's demand is rebate-driven — a risk to underwrite (see §Risks).

Module 3

Map the value chain — the constraint is not "leads"

Chinese OEMs bring product and price (undercutting Japanese brands ~20–30%) but cannot serve local needs from China. The binding constraints on adoption are local infrastructure, not buyer awareness.

What's missing in the system

  • Dealer & after-sales service networks
  • Spare-parts availability
  • Fleet & consumer financing rails
  • A used-EV resale market (depresses residuals, blocks financing)
  • Homologation / type-approval know-how

Implication: "constraint, not category"

Don't sell what's missing on the shelf; own what's missing in the system. The deepest value sits in financing + service + residuals.

A pure lead-gen middleman owns none of these — which is why it is defensible only where it can own a different scarce asset: a neutral, trusted comparison layer for confused retail buyers. That asset exists only where the buyer is a retail consumer — i.e., Malaysia.

Module 5

Sequence entry — the staircase
Stage 1 · Prove

Asset-light, retail: Malaysia-EV or Brazil-ICE

Own the neutral "which 2W to buy" comparison/content layer via SEO + TikTok/FB → WhatsApp → route to dealers. Near-zero paid CAC. Malaysia-EV is the cleanest test; Brazil-ICE the higher-ceiling one (capture the long-tail Chinese brands without networks).

Stage 2 · Build

Turn traffic into an asset

Convert demand into defensibility: dealer relationships, buyer-intent data, a trusted neutral brand. Add financing/insurance affiliate revenue. Prove dealers/brands pay.

Stage 3 · Scale

Graduate to a fleet bet

Graduate into higher-margin fleet-enablement — Brazil-EV/Indonesia fleets, or Africa moto-taxi (+73%). But the Africa deep-dive shows that theatre is integrating fast (Spiro et al. own the stack); the open angle there is a capital-backed financing/data layer, not lead-gen — pursue only with funding.

Commit to the heavy model only after the cheap one produces data. Asset-light retail markets are bounded, so scale lives in the fleet graduation — but Africa is contested capital, not a lead-gen on-ramp. The cleaner graduation is Brazil (where you'd already have ICE relationships) into Brazil-EV fleets.

The chosen theater

LatAm — the open opportunity (deep-dive)

Deep research across Brazil + secondary LatAm (Codex-validated) found the key contrast: LatAm's 2W value chain is structurally open — fragmented importers + multi-brand dealers + open classifieds, with Chinese brands entering through local partnerships — the opposite of Africa's vertical integration. That's the condition a demand-gen layer needs.

The wedge must be re-pointed

  • Leaders self-distribute (Shineray 438 stores; Italika owns Mexico) → your customer is the long tail of newer Chinese entrants lacking demand-gen.
  • Lead value = financing origination, not ad clicks — banks own Brazil's auto classifieds for exactly this reason.
  • The fit: social (TikTok/FB/IG) → WhatsApp-qualification → finance-ready dealer handoff (WhatsApp on 99% of Brazil phones).

Why LatAm beats the alternatives

  • vs Africa: open vs captured — Africa's chain is integrating (Spiro et al. own the stack).
  • vs Malaysia-EV: larger TAM, more open, more mature funnel (WhatsApp commerce, per-lead economy).
  • Recommendation: Brazil #1, Colombia #2, Peru watch.

All LatAm figures Codex-validated (Brazil ~2.2M not 2.35M; Shineray +70% not +80.7%; Mexico tariff ~25% not 35%). Directional thesis holds; data trustworthiness "mixed."

Does the model hold?

Business-model validation — marginal & conversion-gated

A unit-economics model tested whether a viable per-lead price band exists — a price both profitable for us and ROI-positive for the dealer. The answer reframes the whole opportunity.

ScenarioConversionCAC/leadDealer max WTPViable bandVerdict
Base6%$5.00$6.60$5.00–$6.60thin
Pessimistic3%$8.00$3.30EMPTYfails
Optimistic10%$3.00$11.00$3.00–$11.00works

A dealer rationally pays at most conversion × profit-per-sale ÷ ROI-hurdle. On a ~$2,200 bike (~$330 margin) at 6% conversion = $6.60/lead vs our ~$5 CAC — razor-thin; empty below ~4% conversion. Conversion is the single make-or-break variable.

Three ways the economics get un-marginal (the real hypotheses)

1
Sell to brands, not small dealers
Entrants value market-entry + trust above pure ROI → pay above a dealer's hurdle
2
Capture financing-origination
A finance-qualified lead pays twice — dealer and lender. The real moat.
3
Higher-margin bikes
Premium Chinese ($500–1,000+ margin) lift dealer WTP 2–3×

The 90-day, $3–5k kill-or-confirm plan

Phase 1 · Wk 1–3

Demand interviews

15–20 dealer/brand/lender interviews. Kill if <30% will pay in-band.

Phase 2 · Wk 3–9

Concierge MVP

One city, route hand-qualified WhatsApp leads free; measure real lead→sale conversion. Kill if <4%.

Phase 3 · Wk 9–13

Invoice for real money

Charge for the next batch at 2–3 in-band prices. Go if ≥3 pay at positive margin.

The honest read. LatAm is the best shape, but even here the pure asset-light per-lead version is marginal; the viable business is the finance/brand layer (less asset-light). The one thing that decides it — will dealers/brands pay, and do leads convert above ~8%? — is unprovable from a desk. Don't build before the Phase-2 conversion gate passes. What could break this

Risks & open questions to underwrite

Risks

  • Low revenue ceiling. Malaysia EV is tens of thousands of units/yr → lean/lifestyle scale at first. Stage-3 graduation is the upside, not Stage 1.
  • Rebate dependence. Demand is policy-sensitive; MARiiCas lapsed once already.
  • Disintermediation / own-funnel. Blueshark/PETRONAS has retail reach; brands can build their own capture. Defend with neutrality + data.
  • Thin per-lead economics. Only clears with near-zero-CAC organic; paid ads break it.

Open questions (verify before committing capital)

  • Will Malaysian dealers actually pay for leads, and at what price? (Pilot test.)
  • Real EV unit volume in Malaysia (absolute, not just growth %).
  • Does anyone already own "EV motorbike Malaysia" SEO/content? (White-space scan.)
  • CKD-rebate rules — which brands/dealers are incentive-eligible (your lead buyers).
Framework caveat. The load-bearing claim — the model selects the market — should be pressure-tested against a second case before being trusted as a general rule. Here it is internally consistent and decision-useful.
